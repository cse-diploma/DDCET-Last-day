Chapter 01 · Ecosystem
Ecosystem
An ecosystem is a functional unit of nature where living organisms (biotic) interact with each other and with their non-living environment (abiotic) and exchange of matter and energy takes place between them.
🌿 Components of Ecosystem 2 types
Biotic Components (Living)
  • Producers (Autotrophs): Plants, algae — make their own food via photosynthesis
  • Consumers (Heterotrophs): Animals that eat other organisms
    — Primary (herbivores), Secondary (carnivores), Tertiary
  • Decomposers: Fungi, bacteria — break down dead organic matter and return nutrients to soil
Abiotic Components (Non-living)
  • Sunlight — primary energy source
  • Water — essential for all life processes
  • Soil — provides minerals and nutrients
  • Temperature — affects metabolic rates
  • Air (CO₂, O₂, N₂)
  • pH, salinity, pressure
⚡ Energy Flow in Ecosystem Unidirectional
  • 01
    Source: Sun is the ultimate source of energy for all ecosystems on Earth.
  • 02
    Food Chain: Energy flows linearly — Producers → Primary Consumer → Secondary Consumer → Tertiary Consumer → Decomposers.
  • 03
    10% Law (Lindeman, 1942): Only 10% of energy is transferred from one trophic level to the next. Remaining 90% is lost as heat.
  • 04
    Food Web: Interconnected network of multiple food chains in an ecosystem — more realistic representation of energy flow.
  • 05
    Unidirectional: Energy flows only in one direction (Sun → Producers → Consumers). It cannot be recycled like nutrients.
  • 06
    Ecological Pyramids: Graphical representation of trophic levels.
    — Pyramid of Number (no. of organisms)
    — Pyramid of Biomass (total mass at each level)
    — Pyramid of Energy (always upright, never inverted)
🔄 Nutrient (Biogeochemical) Cycles Cyclic
  • 01
    Definition: Cyclic flow of nutrients between biotic and abiotic components of an ecosystem.
  • 02
    Carbon Cycle: CO₂ absorbed by plants (photosynthesis) → released by respiration, decomposition, burning of fossil fuels.
  • 03
    Nitrogen Cycle: N₂ fixed by bacteria → nitrates absorbed by plants → consumed by animals → decomposition returns nitrogen to soil.
  • 04
    Water (Hydrological) Cycle: Evaporation → Condensation → Precipitation → Surface runoff/Groundwater → back to evaporation.
  • 05
    Phosphorus Cycle: No atmospheric phase — moves from rocks to soil to organisms to water and back.
🌐 Types of Ecosystems
Natural Ecosystems
  • Terrestrial: Forest, Grassland, Desert, Tundra
  • Aquatic – Freshwater: Pond, River, Lake, Wetland
  • Aquatic – Marine: Ocean, Sea, Coral reef, Estuary
Artificial (Man-made) Ecosystems
  • Croplands / Agricultural fields
  • Aquarium
  • Garden / Parks
  • Reservoirs / Dams
💡 Exam Tip: Largest ecosystem = Ocean (Marine). Largest terrestrial ecosystem = Tropical Rainforest. Most productive ecosystem = Estuaries and Coral reefs.
Chapter 02 · Pollution
Pollution & Its Types
Pollution is the introduction of harmful substances or energy into the natural environment, causing adverse effects on living organisms, human health, and physical environment.
📖 Key Terms to Know
  • 01
    Pollutant: Any substance or energy that causes pollution. Can be natural (volcanic ash) or man-made (vehicle emissions).
  • 02
    Biodegradable Pollutants: Broken down by microorganisms — e.g., sewage, food waste.
  • 03
    Non-biodegradable Pollutants: Cannot be broken down — e.g., plastic, DDT, heavy metals. Cause biomagnification.
  • 04
    Biomagnification: Increase in concentration of pollutants (e.g., DDT) as they move up the food chain.
  • 05
    Eutrophication: Excessive nutrient (N, P) in water causes algal bloom → depletes oxygen → kills aquatic life.
⚠️ Types of Pollution — Complete Reference 6 types
Type Major Causes / Sources Key Effects Control Measures
🌫️ Air Vehicles, industries, burning fossil fuels, agricultural burning, power plants Smog, acid rain, respiratory diseases, ozone depletion, global warming CNG vehicles, scrubbers, afforestation, renewable energy
💧 Water Industrial effluents, sewage, agricultural runoff (pesticides, fertilizers), oil spills, plastic Waterborne diseases (cholera, typhoid), eutrophication, aquatic biodiversity loss, dead zones Sewage treatment plants (STP), banning single-use plastic, organic farming
🌱 Soil / Land Pesticides, chemical fertilizers, solid waste, mining, industrial waste, landfills Loss of fertility, groundwater contamination, food chain toxicity, reduced microbial activity Organic farming, proper waste disposal, bioremediation, crop rotation
🔊 Noise Vehicles, aircraft, construction, industries, loudspeakers, blasting Hearing loss, stress, hypertension, sleep disturbance, wildlife behavioral disruption Soundproofing, noise barriers, laws on decibel limits, green belts
☢️ Radioactive Nuclear power plants, weapons testing, uranium mining, medical/industrial radioactive waste Cancer, genetic mutations, radiation sickness, long-term land contamination Shielding, safe storage of nuclear waste, strict regulations
🌡️ Thermal Discharge of heated water from power plants and industries into rivers and lakes Reduced dissolved oxygen, fish kills, algal bloom, disrupts aquatic ecosystems Cooling towers, recycling heated water, heat recovery systems
💡 Exam Tips: Minamata disease = Mercury poisoning (water). Itai-itai = Cadmium poisoning. Black lung disease = Coal dust (air). Noise above 85 dB is harmful. SPM = Suspended Particulate Matter in air. BOD = Biological Oxygen Demand (measures water pollution).
🌧️ Acid Rain — Important Topic
  • 01
    Cause: SO₂ and NO₂ released by industries and vehicles react with water vapour in atmosphere.
  • 02
    Chemistry: SO₂ + H₂O → H₂SO₃ (Sulphurous acid); NO₂ + H₂O → HNO₃ (Nitric acid).
  • 03
    pH: Normal rain pH = 5.6. Acid rain pH < 5.6.
  • 04
    Effects: Damages forests, corrodes buildings (marble cancer), acidifies lakes, kills fish, damages crops.
  • 05
    Marble Cancer: Acid rain dissolves CaCO₃ in marble monuments — e.g., Taj Mahal damage.
Chapter 03 · Climate Change
Climate Change
Climate change refers to long-term shifts in global temperatures and weather patterns, primarily caused by human activities since the Industrial Revolution (mid-18th century).
🌍 Greenhouse Effect & Global Warming
  • 01
    Natural Greenhouse Effect: Certain atmospheric gases trap solar radiation, keeping Earth ~33°C warmer than it would otherwise be. Essential for life.
  • 02
    Enhanced Greenhouse Effect: Human activities increase GHG concentrations → more heat trapped → global warming.
  • 03
    Key Greenhouse Gases (GHGs):
    — CO₂ (Carbon dioxide) — from burning fossil fuels. Most significant GHG.
    — CH₄ (Methane) — from livestock, rice paddies, landfills. 25× more potent than CO₂.
    — N₂O (Nitrous oxide) — from fertilizers. 298× more potent than CO₂.
    — CFCs — from refrigerants, aerosols. Also destroys ozone layer.
    — Water vapour — natural amplifier of warming.
  • 04
    Global Warming Potential (GWP): Measure of how much heat a GHG traps compared to CO₂ over 100 years.
  • 05
    Current Status: Earth's average temperature has risen by ~1.1°C above pre-industrial levels. Paris Agreement (2015) aims to limit it to 1.5°C.
🔥 Causes of Climate Change
  • 01
    Burning of Fossil Fuels: Coal, oil, natural gas release CO₂ and SO₂ — biggest contributor to GHG emissions.
  • 02
    Deforestation: Trees absorb CO₂; cutting them reduces carbon sinks and releases stored carbon.
  • 03
    Agriculture: Livestock (enteric fermentation) → CH₄; paddy fields → CH₄; fertilizers → N₂O.
  • 04
    Industrialization: Factories emit CO₂, CH₄, and fluorinated gases during manufacturing.
  • 05
    Transportation: Cars, trucks, aircraft, ships burn fossil fuels → CO₂ + NO₂ emissions.
  • 06
    Waste & Landfills: Decomposing organic waste in landfills produces large amounts of CH₄.
  • 07
    Urbanization: Urban Heat Islands (UHI) — cities are warmer than surrounding areas due to concrete, vehicles, and reduced vegetation.
🌊 Effects of Climate Change
  • 01
    Rising Sea Levels: Melting glaciers and thermal expansion of water → flooding of coastal cities and islands.
  • 02
    Melting Ice Caps: Arctic and Antarctic ice shrinking → loss of habitat for polar bears, penguins.
  • 03
    Extreme Weather Events: More intense and frequent hurricanes, floods, droughts, heatwaves, and wildfires.
  • 04
    Ocean Acidification: CO₂ dissolves in ocean → forms carbonic acid → harms coral reefs and marine life.
  • 05
    Loss of Biodiversity: Species unable to adapt go extinct; coral bleaching; migration of species to cooler areas.
  • 06
    Food & Water Insecurity: Changed rainfall patterns and droughts affect agriculture and freshwater availability.
  • 07
    Human Health: Spread of vector-borne diseases (malaria, dengue), heat strokes, malnutrition.
  • 08
    Ozone Layer Depletion: CFCs destroy stratospheric ozone → increased UV radiation → skin cancer, cataracts.
💡 Exam Tip: Ozone layer is in the stratosphere (15–35 km). Its depletion is detected via "ozone hole" over Antarctica. Montreal Protocol (1987) bans CFCs. IPCC = Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change.
🛡️ Mitigation & Adaptation
Mitigation (Reduce the Cause)
  • Switch to renewable energy (solar, wind)
  • Improve energy efficiency (LED, insulation)
  • Carbon capture and storage (CCS)
  • Afforestation and reforestation
  • Reduce, Reuse, Recycle
  • Electric vehicles (EVs), public transport
  • International agreements (Paris, Kyoto)
Adaptation (Deal with the Effect)
  • Build flood defences and sea walls
  • Develop drought-resistant crops
  • Improve disaster early-warning systems
  • Urban green spaces to reduce heat islands
  • Diversify water sources (rainwater harvesting)
  • Climate-resilient infrastructure
  • Protect and restore mangroves and wetlands
Chapter 04 · Renewable Energy
Renewable Sources of Energy
Renewable energy is energy from sources that are naturally replenished on a human timescale — such as sunlight, wind, rain, tides, waves, and geothermal heat. They are inexhaustible and produce little to no GHG emissions.
💧 1. Hydropower (Hydro Energy) Flowing Water
  • 01
    Principle: Kinetic and potential energy of flowing or falling water is used to rotate turbines, generating electricity.
  • 02
    Types: Large dams (e.g., Bhakra Nangal), Run-of-river plants, Pumped storage hydropower, Small/micro hydro.
  • 03
    Availability: Requires rivers with sufficient flow and elevation difference. Abundant in hilly/mountainous regions.
  • 04
    ✅ Advantages: Large-scale generation, no fuel cost, long lifespan (50–100 years), can quickly respond to demand, provides flood control and irrigation.
  • 05
    ❌ Limitations: Displaces communities, floods large areas, disrupts aquatic ecosystems, dam failure risk, dependent on rainfall (drought-sensitive), high construction cost, sedimentation reduces capacity over time.
  • 06
    Examples: Three Gorges Dam (China) — world's largest. Bhakra Nangal (India). Hoover Dam (USA).
☀️ 2. Solar Energy Sunlight
  • 01
    Principle: Solar energy is captured using photovoltaic (PV) cells or solar thermal systems.
    — PV cells: Convert sunlight directly into electricity (photoelectric effect).
    — Solar thermal: Use sunlight to heat water/steam to drive turbines.
  • 02
    Applications: Solar panels (homes/commercial), solar water heaters, solar cookers, solar lanterns, concentrated solar power (CSP) plants.
  • 03
    Availability: Globally available; best in tropical and desert regions (India has ~300 sunny days/year). India's solar target: 500 GW by 2030.
  • 04
    ✅ Advantages: Infinite source, zero emissions during operation, low maintenance, scalable (from rooftop to utility), rapidly falling costs (cheapest electricity source now in many regions).
  • 05
    ❌ Limitations: Intermittent (no output at night or during heavy cloud cover), needs large land area for utility-scale plants, requires battery storage, PV manufacturing involves toxic chemicals.
  • 06
    India: Bhadla Solar Park (Rajasthan) — world's largest solar park.
🌬️ 3. Wind Energy Wind Turbines
  • 01
    Principle: Kinetic energy of wind rotates turbine blades → shaft rotates → generator produces electricity.
  • 02
    Types:
    — Onshore: Turbines on land; more common and cheaper.
    — Offshore: Turbines in sea; stronger/consistent winds, less visual impact, but more expensive.
  • 03
    Availability: Best in coastal areas, plains, mountain passes. Wind speed > 6 m/s is generally needed for viability.
  • 04
    ✅ Advantages: No fuel cost, no water use, minimal land footprint (farming can continue around turbines), low emissions, fast deployment.
  • 05
    ❌ Limitations: Intermittent and variable (wind-dependent), noise and visual pollution, bird and bat mortality, not suitable for all regions, high initial cost for offshore.
  • 06
    India: Tamil Nadu, Rajasthan, Gujarat are top wind energy states. India = 4th largest wind energy producer globally.
🌾 4. Biomass Energy Organic Matter
  • 01
    Definition: Energy derived from organic materials — plant matter, agricultural residue, animal waste, food waste, and wood.
  • 02
    Forms of Biomass Energy:
    — Direct burning: Wood, crop residue for heat/electricity.
    — Biogas: CH₄ produced from decomposition of organic waste in biogas plants (anaerobic digestion).
    — Biofuels: Ethanol (from sugarcane/corn) and biodiesel (from vegetable oils) for vehicles.
    — Biochar: Carbon-rich charcoal that improves soil and stores carbon.
  • 03
    Availability: Widely available worldwide, especially in agricultural economies. India has high biomass potential from agricultural waste.
  • 04
    ✅ Advantages: Utilizes waste material, carbon-neutral cycle (CO₂ released = CO₂ absorbed during growth), can produce electricity + heat + liquid fuel, reduces waste in landfills.
  • 05
    ❌ Limitations: Burning can release air pollutants (CO, particulates), risk of deforestation if biomass is not sustainably sourced, competes with food crops (biofuels vs food), lower energy efficiency than fossil fuels.
🌊 5. Tidal Energy Ocean Tides
  • 01
    Principle: Tidal energy harnesses the kinetic and potential energy from the rise and fall of ocean tides, caused by gravitational pull of the Moon and Sun.
  • 02
    Technologies:
    — Tidal Barrages: Dam-like structure across tidal estuary; water flows through turbines as tide rises/falls.
    — Tidal Stream Generators: Underwater turbines in fast tidal currents (like underwater wind turbines).
    — Tidal Lagoons: Enclosed areas of sea with turbines in walls.
  • 03
    Availability: Limited to coastal areas with large tidal range (>5 m). Best sites: English Channel, Bay of Fundy (Canada), Gulf of Kutch (India).
  • 04
    ✅ Advantages: Highly predictable (astronomically determined), no fuel cost, long lifespan, zero GHG emissions during operation, reliable twice-daily output.
  • 05
    ❌ Limitations: Very limited suitable locations globally, high construction and maintenance costs, disrupts marine ecosystems and fish migration, output not continuous (only during tidal movement), still largely an emerging technology.
🌋 6. Geothermal Energy Earth's Heat
  • 01
    Principle: Heat from Earth's interior (from radioactive decay of elements and residual heat from Earth's formation) is tapped to produce electricity or direct heating.
  • 02
    How it works: Wells are drilled into geothermal reservoirs → steam/hot water rises to surface → drives turbines → generates electricity. Spent steam is condensed and returned.
  • 03
    Availability: Geographically limited to tectonically active regions — volcanoes, geysers, plate boundaries. Best: Iceland, USA (The Geysers), Philippines, Kenya, Italy. India: Puga Valley (Ladakh), Tattapani.
  • 04
    ✅ Advantages: Continuous 24/7 output (not intermittent), small land footprint, low GHG emissions, low operating costs once drilled, can be used for both electricity and direct heating (buildings, greenhouses).
  • 05
    ❌ Limitations: Geographically restricted, very high drilling costs, risk of releasing H₂S (toxic gas), can trigger minor earthquakes (enhanced geothermal systems), may deplete if heat extracted faster than replenished.
  • 06
    Iceland: Gets ~90% of heating and ~30% of electricity from geothermal — best example in the world.
