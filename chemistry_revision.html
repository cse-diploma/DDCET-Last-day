Chapter 1 — Chemical Reactions & Equations
⚗️
Writing & Balancing Equations
What is a Chemical Equation?
  • Symbolic representation of a chemical reaction
  • Reactants written on LEFT, products on RIGHT, arrow (→) in between
  • State symbols: (s) solid, (l) liquid, (g) gas, (aq) aqueous
  • ↑ = gas evolved, ↓ = precipitate formed
💡
Balancing Rule: Total atoms of each element must be equal on both sides (Law of Conservation of Mass)
Steps to Balance an Equation
  • Write the skeleton equation first
  • Count atoms on each side
  • Add coefficients (not subscripts) to balance
  • Verify: atoms balanced on both sides
  • Add state symbols at the end
⚠️
Never change subscripts to balance — only add coefficients before formulas!
🔄
Types of Chemical Reactions
1️⃣  Combination Reaction A + B → AB ▼

Two or more substances combine to form a single product.

Burning of magnesium
2Mg(s) + O₂(g) → 2MgO(s)
Magnesium burns with dazzling white light → magnesium oxide (white powder)
Formation of water
2H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2H₂O(l)
Quicklime + water (Exothermic)
CaO(s) + H₂O(l) → Ca(OH)₂(aq) + Heat
Slaking of lime — exothermic reaction
Burning of coal
C(s) + O₂(g) → CO₂(g)
💡
Many combination reactions are exothermic (release heat). Combustion is always exothermic!
2️⃣  Decomposition Reaction AB → A + B ▼

A single compound breaks into two or more simpler substances. Opposite of combination. Requires energy (heat/light/electricity).

Thermal decomp. of Ferrous sulphate (TEST-FAVORITE!)
2FeSO₄(s) →(heat)→ Fe₂O₃(s) + SO₂(g) + SO₃(g)
Green FeSO₄ → reddish-brown Fe₂O₃ (smell of burning sulphur)
Decomp. of Lead nitrate
2Pb(NO₃)₂(s) →(heat)→ 2PbO(s) + 4NO₂(g) + O₂(g)
Brown fumes of NO₂ evolved
Decomp. of Silver chloride (Photolysis)
2AgCl(s) →(sunlight)→ 2Ag(s) + Cl₂(g)
Basis of black-and-white photography. AgBr also same.
Electrolysis of water
2H₂O(l) →(electricity)→ 2H₂(g) + O₂(g)
Decomp. of Calcium carbonate (Calcination)
CaCO₃(s) →(heat)→ CaO(s) + CO₂(g)
Limestone → Quicklime. Industrial process.
💡
Types: Thermal (heat), Photolytic (light), Electrolytic (electricity)
3️⃣  Displacement Reaction A + BC → AC + B ▼

More reactive element displaces less reactive element from its compound.

Iron + Copper Sulphate (Classic!)
Fe(s) + CuSO₄(aq) → FeSO₄(aq) + Cu(s)
Blue solution fades; reddish copper deposits on iron nail
Zinc + Copper sulphate
Zn(s) + CuSO₄(aq) → ZnSO₄(aq) + Cu(s)
Zinc + dilute H₂SO₄
Zn(s) + H₂SO₄(aq) → ZnSO₄(aq) + H₂(g)↑
⚠️
Key rule: More reactive metal displaces less reactive metal. Learn the reactivity series!
4️⃣  Double Displacement AB + CD → AD + CB ▼

Exchange of ions between two compounds. Usually results in precipitate formation.

Precipitation of BaSO₄ (WHITE PPT)
BaCl₂(aq) + Na₂SO₄(aq) → BaSO₄(s)↓ + 2NaCl(aq)
White precipitate of barium sulphate — insoluble in dil. HCl
Precipitation of PbSO₄
Pb(NO₃)₂(aq) + 2KI(aq) → PbI₂(s)↓ + 2KNO₃(aq)
Yellow precipitate of lead iodide
Neutralisation
NaOH(aq) + HCl(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l)
Acid + Base → Salt + Water (always!)
💡
Double displacement reactions forming precipitate = precipitation reactions. Neutralisation is also double displacement.
⚡ Oxidation & Reduction (Bonus)
Oxidation
  • Gain of oxygen
  • Loss of hydrogen
  • Loss of electrons (OIL)
Reduction
  • Loss of oxygen
  • Gain of hydrogen
  • Gain of electrons (RIG)
Memory Trick
OIL RIG
Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain (of electrons). In every redox reaction, both occur simultaneously.
🔁
Redox Example: CuO + H₂ → Cu + H₂O  |  CuO is reduced (loses O), H₂ is oxidised (gains O)
🌫️
Rancidity: Oxidation of fats and oils → bad smell. Prevented by: antioxidants, nitrogen packing, refrigeration, airtight containers.
🟤
Corrosion: Oxidation of metals (iron rusts, silver tarnishes, copper turns green). Fe + O₂ + H₂O → Fe₂O₃·xH₂O (rust)
Chapter 2 — Acids, Bases & Salts
🧪
Chemical Properties
Properties of Acids
  • Sour taste (never taste in lab!)
  • Turn blue litmus RED
  • pH < 7
  • React with metals → salt + H₂↑
  • React with bases → salt + water
  • Conduct electricity (ionise in water)
  • Contain H⁺ ions in solution
Properties of Bases
  • Bitter taste, soapy feel
  • Turn red litmus BLUE
  • pH > 7
  • React with acids → salt + water
  • React with non-metal oxides
  • Conduct electricity
  • Contain OH⁻ ions in solution
🔬 All Important Reactions — Acids & Bases
Acids + Metals▼
Zinc + dilute H₂SO₄
Zn + H₂SO₄ → ZnSO₄ + H₂↑
Iron + dilute HCl
Fe + 2HCl → FeCl₂ + H₂↑
Magnesium + dilute HCl
Mg + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂↑
Mg reacts vigorously, bubbles rapidly
⚠️
Copper does NOT react with dilute HCl (low in reactivity series)
Acids + Metal Carbonates / Bicarbonates▼
Sodium carbonate + HCl
Na₂CO₃ + 2HCl → 2NaCl + H₂O + CO₂↑
Sodium bicarbonate + HCl
NaHCO₃ + HCl → NaCl + H₂O + CO₂↑
CO₂ turns lime water milky: Ca(OH)₂ + CO₂ → CaCO₃↓ + H₂O
Lime Water Test
Ca(OH)₂ + CO₂ → CaCO₃↓ + H₂O
White precipitate = CO₂ present. Excess CO₂ makes it clear again (CaCO₃ → Ca(HCO₃)₂)
Reaction of Metallic Oxides with Acids▼

Metal oxides are basic in nature. They react with acids to form salt + water.

Copper oxide + dilute HCl
CuO + 2HCl → CuCl₂ + H₂O
Black CuO dissolves → blue-green CuCl₂ solution
Copper oxide + H₂SO₄
CuO + H₂SO₄ → CuSO₄ + H₂O
General Formula
Metal Oxide + Acid → Salt + Water
Metal oxides = basic oxides. Non-metal oxides (SO₂, CO₂) are acidic oxides.
Acids & Bases in Water — Ionisation▼
HCl in water
HCl + H₂O → H₃O⁺ + Cl⁻
H⁺ (proton) attaches to water → hydronium ion H₃O⁺. This is what makes it acidic.
NaOH in water
NaOH → Na⁺ + OH⁻
OH⁻ is responsible for basic character
💡
Acids without water do NOT show acidic properties (no H⁺ ions). Pure HCl gas won't turn litmus red — only HCl solution will!

Importance of pH
  • Tooth decay: Bacteria produce acid; pH drops below 5.5 → enamel corrodes
  • Stomach: HCl (pH~2) aids digestion; excess → antacids (Mg(OH)₂)
  • Plants: Best growth at pH 6–7; acidic/basic soil treated with lime/fertilisers
  • Bee sting: Acidic → apply baking soda
  • Ant bite: Formic acid → baking soda neutralises
  • Wasp sting: Alkaline → apply vinegar
  • Blood: pH 7.35–7.45; variation = enzyme malfunction

Baking soda → Baking powder
NaHCO₃ + heat → Na₂CO₃ + H₂O + CO₂↑
Baking powder = NaHCO₃ + tartaric acid (prevents soapy taste of Na₂CO₃)

2NaCl + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + Cl₂↑ + H₂↑

(CaSO₄)·½H₂O + 1½H₂O → CaSO₄·2H₂O
Gypsum forms when POP sets — expands slightly

Chapter 3 — Metals & Non-metals
⚙️
Physical Properties
Metals
  • Lustre — Shiny surface
  • Malleable — Can be hammered into sheets (Gold most malleable)
  • Ductile — Can be drawn into wires (Gold most ductile)
  • Good conductors of heat & electricity
  • High melting point (except Ga, Cs, Hg)
  • Sonorous — Produce sound when struck
  • Hard (except Na, K — soft, cut with knife)
  • High density (except Li, Na, K — float on water)
Non-metals
  • No lustre (except iodine, graphite)
  • Brittle in solid state
  • Non-malleable, non-ductile
  • Poor conductors (except graphite)
  • Low melting point (except diamond)
  • Not sonorous
  • Low density
  • Some are gases at room temp (O₂, N₂, Cl₂)
⚡
Graphite — only non-metal that conducts electricity (used in electrodes)
⚗️ Chemical Properties of Metals
Reaction of Metals with Oxygen▼
Magnesium (burns brightly)
2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO (basic oxide)
Sodium (reacts vigorously)
4Na + O₂ → 2Na₂O
Na stored in kerosene to prevent oxidation
Iron (rusting — slow oxidation)
4Fe + 3O₂ + xH₂O → 2Fe₂O₃·xH₂O (rust)
Gold, Silver, Platinum
Do NOT react with O₂ (least reactive)
Noble metals — remain shiny
💡
Metal oxides are basic. Non-metal oxides are acidic (SO₂, CO₂, NO₂).
Reaction of Metals with Water▼
Sodium + cold water (vigorous)
2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂↑
Reacts explosively; H₂ catches fire
Calcium + cold water
Ca + 2H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ + H₂↑
Less vigorous than Na; milky solution
Magnesium + hot water / steam
Mg + H₂O (steam) → MgO + H₂↑
Reacts only with steam, not cold water
Iron + steam (very hot)
3Fe + 4H₂O (steam) → Fe₃O₄ + 4H₂↑
Copper
No reaction with water or steam
Reaction of Metals with Dilute Acids▼
General equation
Metal + Dilute acid → Salt + H₂↑
Zinc + HCl
Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂↑
Magnesium + H₂SO₄
Mg + H₂SO₄ → MgSO₄ + H₂↑
⚠️
Copper, Silver, Gold do NOT react with dilute acids. They need concentrated HNO₃ (aqua regia for gold).

🔗 Metal + Non-metal → Ionic Compound
Formation of NaCl
Sodium gives electron to Chlorine
Na → Na⁺ + e⁻ (oxidation)
Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻ (reduction)
Na⁺ + Cl⁻ → NaCl (ionic bond)
Properties of Ionic Compounds
  • Solid at room temperature, hard and brittle
  • High melting and boiling points
  • Conduct electricity in molten/aqueous state (not solid)
  • Soluble in water (polar solvents)
  • Insoluble in organic solvents (petrol, kerosene)
🟤 Corrosion of Metals
Key Corrosion Facts
  • Rusting of iron: Fe + O₂ + H₂O → Fe₂O₃·xH₂O
  • Silver tarnishes: Ag₂S (black) — reacts with H₂S in air
  • Copper turns green: CuCO₃·Cu(OH)₂ (green patina)
  • Corrosion weakens structural material
Prevention of Corrosion
  • Painting / Oiling / Greasing — barrier from air/water
  • Galvanisation — coat iron with Zinc
  • Electroplating — deposit metal layer
  • Alloying — stainless steel (Fe+Cr+Ni) doesn't rust
  • Cathodic protection — use sacrificial anode
🔥 Thermite Reaction (Displacement)
Aluminium displaces iron (very exothermic!)
Fe₂O₃ + 2Al → Al₂O₃ + 2Fe + Large Heat
Used for welding railway tracks (thermite welding). Al is more reactive than Fe.
💡
This is a displacement reaction AND redox reaction — Al is oxidised, Fe₂O₃ is reduced.

Common 1-Mark Questions
  • What gas is evolved when zinc reacts with HCl? → H₂
  • Which metal is stored in kerosene? → Sodium
  • Which non-metal conducts electricity? → Graphite
  • pH of neutral solution? → 7
  • Which acid is in ant's sting? → Formic acid (HCOOH)
  • Bee sting — acid or base? → Acidic (use NaHCO₃)
  • Wasp sting — acid or base? → Basic/Alkaline (use vinegar)
  • Chemical name of baking soda? → Sodium bicarbonate
  • Chemical name of washing soda? → Sodium carbonate decahydrate
  • POP full form? → Plaster of Paris
  • Most ductile metal? → Gold
  • Which metal is liquid at room temp? → Mercury (Hg)
Common 2-Mark / Reasoning Questions
  • Why does rusting need both O₂ and water? → Both needed to form Fe₂O₃·xH₂O
  • Why is Na stored in kerosene? → Reacts vigorously with O₂ and H₂O in air
  • Why copper used in electrical wires? → Good conductor + ductile (not because most ductile — that's gold)
  • Why is pure HCl gas not acidic? → No H⁺ ions without water
  • Why does AgCl turn grey in sunlight? → Photolytic decomposition; Ag formed
  • Why are ionic compounds solid? → Strong electrostatic forces between ions
  • Why does colour of CuSO₄ change when Fe dipped? → Fe more reactive, displaces Cu

⚠️ Tricky Points — Don't Make These Mistakes!
❌
Copper does NOT react with dilute HCl or H₂SO₄ (below H in reactivity series)
❌
Don't say "photolysis of AgCl produces silver and oxygen" — it produces Ag and Cl₂
❌
Graphite is a non-metal that conducts electricity. Don't say "all non-metals are insulators"
❌
Don't confuse Baking Soda (NaHCO₃) and Washing Soda (Na₂CO₃·10H₂O)
❌
Plaster of Paris is CaSO₄·½H₂O, NOT CaSO₄·2H₂O (that's gypsum)
❌
pH scale is 0–14, not 1–14. Strong acids can have pH = 0 or even negative!
✅ Last-Minute Golden Tips
✅
Always write state symbols — (s), (l), (g), (aq) — in reactions for full marks
✅
Write conditions above the arrow (heat Δ, light hν, electricity) when applicable
✅
For displacement: always check reactivity series — MORE reactive element displaces LESS reactive
✅
Name the type of reaction whenever asked — 1 extra mark in many questions
✅
Mention colour change in observations (blue → pale green for CuSO₄ with Fe)
✅
For pH questions: lower pH = stronger acid, higher pH = stronger base. pH 7 = neutral
✅
Revise the reactivity series mnemonic — at least 5 reactions can be predicted from it
✅
In 5-mark questions: define the concept → give equation → state conditions → observation → application
