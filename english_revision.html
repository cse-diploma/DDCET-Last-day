Theory of Communication
Definition
  • Communication = process of exchanging information, ideas, feelings between sender and receiver
  • Latin root: communis = "to share" or "common"
  • Requires: Sender → Message → Channel → Receiver → Feedback
  • Effective only when message is understood as intended
Elements of the Process
  • Sender — Encodes and initiates message
  • Message — Information to be communicated
  • Encoding — Converting idea into words/symbols
  • Channel/Medium — Path (verbal, written, visual)
  • Receiver — Decodes the message
  • Decoding — Interpreting the message
  • Feedback — Response from receiver to sender
  • Noise — Any interference/barrier
💡
Flow: Sender → Encoding → Message → Channel → Decoding → Receiver → Feedback → (back to Sender). This is a circular process, not one-way.
Verbal vs Non-Verbal Communication
Verbal Communication
  • Uses words — spoken or written
  • Oral: face-to-face, phone, meetings, speeches
  • Written: letters, emails, reports, memos
  • Can be formal or informal
  • Clear, precise, recorded (written form)
Examples
Speech, interview, presentation, email, notice, report, letter
Non-Verbal Communication
  • Without words — uses body language, signs, symbols
  • Kinesics: Body movements, gestures, posture
  • Proxemics: Use of space/distance
  • Paralanguage: Tone, pitch, speed of voice
  • Chronemics: Use of time (punctuality)
  • Haptics: Touch (handshake)
  • Oculesics: Eye contact
  • Reinforces or contradicts verbal message
    • 📊
    Mehrabian's Rule (55-38-7): 55% Body language, 38% Tone of voice, 7% Words. Non-verbal communication carries the most weight!
    Barriers to Communication
    Types of Barriers
    TypeExamples
    PhysicalNoise, distance, poor lighting, technical faults
    PsychologicalEmotions, prejudice, stress, poor listening, distrust
    LanguageJargon, accent, wrong words, ambiguity
    CulturalDifferent customs, values, beliefs
    OrganisationalToo many levels of hierarchy, information overload
    SemanticWords with multiple meanings, wrong interpretation
    How to Overcome Barriers
    • Use simple, clear language — avoid jargon
    • Choose the right channel for the message
    • Active listening — give full attention
    • Seek feedback to confirm understanding
    • Control emotions before communicating
    • Be aware of cultural differences
    • Reduce physical noise and distractions
    • Use proper body language
    ⚠️
    Exam favourite: "What are barriers to communication? Give examples." Always mention at least 4 types with one example each for full marks.
    Parts of a Business Letter
    Standard Layout (Top → Bottom)
    1. Sender's Address — Your full address (top left)
    2. Date — 1st May, 2026 / 01-05-2026
    3. Receiver's Address — Name, designation, company, address
    4. Subject — Brief one-line purpose (Sub: ...)
    5. Salutation — Dear Sir/Madam, Dear Mr./Ms. [Surname]
    6. Body — Para 1: Purpose | Para 2: Details | Para 3: Close
    7. Complimentary Close — Yours faithfully / Yours sincerely
    8. Signature — Name + Designation
    9. Enclosures (if any) — Encl: Invoice / Catalogue
    💡
    "Yours faithfully" — when you don't know the person's name (used with "Dear Sir/Madam"). "Yours sincerely" — when you know the name (used with "Dear Mr./Ms. Sharma").
    Parts of an Email
    Email Structure
    To: receiver@email.com
    Cc: copy to others (Carbon Copy)
    Bcc: Blind Carbon Copy (hidden from others)
    Subject: Clear and specific (Re: Order No. 501)
    Salutation: Dear [Name],
    Body: Short paragraphs — purpose, details, request
    Closing: Regards / Best regards / Yours sincerely
    Signature: Name, Designation, Contact
    Types of Business Letters
    1. Letter of Inquiry Asking for info▼
    • Written to ask for information about products, services, prices, terms
    • Must be polite, specific about what you want to know
    • Request catalogue, price list, samples, delivery terms
    Opening phrase
    "We are interested in purchasing... and would like to know the prices and terms of delivery."
    Close with: "We look forward to your early reply."
    Key vocabulary
    Kindly furnish / Please provide / We would be grateful / We request / enquire / quotation / catalogue / terms of payment
    2. Reply to Inquiry Answering questions▼
    • Written in response to an inquiry letter
    • Acknowledge the inquiry with date and reference
    • Provide all requested information clearly
    • Offer discounts, delivery terms, payment options
    • End with hope for future business
    Opening phrase
    "Thank you for your inquiry dated 20th April. We are pleased to inform you that..."
    Close with: "We hope this information is satisfactory and look forward to receiving your order."
    3. Order Letter Placing an order▼
    • Written to place an order for goods or services
    • Mention: item name, quantity, size/colour, price agreed, payment method, delivery date
    • Reference any previous inquiry or catalogue
    Opening phrase
    "With reference to your quotation dated..., we are pleased to place the following order:"
    Order format
    Sr. No. | Item | Quantity | Rate | Total
    Always give a table or clear list of items ordered
    4. Complaint Letter Raising a problem▼
    • Written when goods/services received are unsatisfactory
    • State the problem clearly: wrong items, damaged goods, delay, short supply
    • Give order number, date, invoice number
    • State what action you expect (replacement, refund, explanation)
    • Firm but polite — never rude
    Opening phrase
    "We regret to inform you that the goods received against our Order No. ___ dated ___ were found to be damaged/incorrect."
    Close with: "We request you to replace the goods at the earliest / issue a credit note."
    ⚠️
    A complaint letter must be specific: give exact order numbers, dates, quantities. Vague complaints get zero marks in exams!
    5. Adjustment Letter Resolving a complaint▼
    • Reply to a complaint letter — written by the seller
    • Acknowledge the complaint and apologise sincerely
    • Explain the reason (if any) without making excuses
    • State the action taken: replacement, refund, credit note
    • Assure it won't happen again — goodwill message at end
    Opening phrase
    "We have received your complaint dated ___ and sincerely regret the inconvenience caused. After investigating the matter, we are pleased to inform you that..."
    Close with: "We assure you of our best services in future."

    Parts of Speech — Quick Reference
    1. Noun — Name of a person/place/thing/idea
    • Common: boy, city, book
    • Proper: Mumbai, Krish, India (capital letters)
    • Collective: team, flock, bunch, committee
    • Abstract: love, honesty, freedom, beauty
    • Countable: can be counted (pen, apple)
    • Uncountable: cannot be counted (milk, water, advice)
    💡
    Advice, information, furniture, luggage = uncountable. Never say "advices" or "informations"!
    2. Pronoun — Replaces a noun
    • Personal: I, we, you, he, she, it, they
    • Reflexive: myself, yourself, themselves
    • Demonstrative: this, that, these, those
    • Interrogative: who, whom, which, what
    • Relative: who, whom, which, that
    • Indefinite: someone, anybody, none, all
    ⚠️
    "Who" = subject. "Whom" = object. "Who called?" ✓ "Whom did you call?" ✓
    3. Adjective — Describes a noun
    • Qualitative: beautiful, large, cold
    • Quantitative: some, much, little, enough
    • Numeral: one, first, many, few
    • Possessive: my, his, her, their
    • Demonstrative: this, that, these, those
    Degrees of Comparison
    Positive: tall | Comparative: taller | Superlative: tallest
    Irregular: good → better → best | bad → worse → worst
    4. Adverb — Modifies verb/adjective/adverb
    • Manner: quickly, slowly, carefully
    • Time: now, yesterday, soon, already
    • Place: here, there, everywhere
    • Frequency: always, never, often, rarely
    • Degree: very, quite, too, enough, almost
    💡
    Most adverbs formed by adding -ly to adjective: quick→quickly. But "fast", "hard", "late", "early" are same as adjective and adverb!
    5. Verb — Action / State of being
    • Action: run, write, speak, eat
    • Linking: is, am, are, was, were, seem, become
    • Auxiliary (Helping): have, has, had, do, does, did, will, shall, can, may, must, should
    • Transitive: needs an object (She reads a book)
    • Intransitive: no object needed (She sleeps)
    • Modal: can, could, may, might, shall, should, will, would, must
    6. Preposition — Shows relationship
    • Place: in, on, at, under, above, below, beside
    • Time: at (3pm), on (Monday), in (March/2026)
    • Direction: to, into, toward, from, through
    • Cause: because of, due to, owing to
    at / on / in — Time Rule
    AT: exact time (at 5pm, at noon) | ON: day/date (on Monday) | IN: month/year/season (in April, in 2026)
    7. Connectors / Conjunctions
    • Coordinating: for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so → FANBOYS
    • Subordinating: because, although, when, since, if, unless, until, after, before
    • Correlative: either…or | neither…nor | both…and | not only…but also
    • Transitional: however, therefore, moreover, furthermore, consequently
    8. Interjection — Sudden emotion
    • Expresses sudden feeling — always followed by ! or ,
    • Joy: Hurrah! Wow! Bravo!
    • Sorrow: Alas! Oh!
    • Surprise: What! Really! Indeed!
    • Approval: Bravo! Well done!
    • Disgust: Ugh! Yuck!
    Tenses — Complete Table
    TenseStructureExampleSignal Words
    Simple Present V1 / V1+s/es She writes letters. always, usually, often, every day
    Present Continuous is/am/are + V-ing She is writing a letter. now, at this moment, currently
    Present Perfect has/have + V3 She has written the letter. just, already, yet, ever, never, since, for
    Present Perfect Continuous has/have been + V-ing She has been writing since morning. since, for + ongoing action
    Simple Past V2 (past form) She wrote a letter. yesterday, ago, last week, in 2010
    Past Continuous was/were + V-ing She was writing when I called. while, when, at that time
    Past Perfect had + V3 She had written before he arrived. before, after, by the time, already
    Past Perfect Continuous had been + V-ing She had been writing for an hour. for, since + past reference point
    Simple Future will/shall + V1 She will write tomorrow. tomorrow, next week, soon, in future
    Future Continuous will be + V-ing She will be writing at 5pm. at (time) tomorrow, this time next week
    Future Perfect will have + V3 She will have written by noon. by (time), before (future point)
    Future Perfect Continuous will have been + V-ing She will have been writing for 2 hours by then. for + duration + by + future point
    💡
    V1 = base form (write), V2 = past simple (wrote), V3 = past participle (written), V-ing = present participle (writing)
    Subject-Verb Agreement — Rules
    Core Rules
    • Singular subject → singular verb: He runs.
    • Plural subject → plural verb: They run.
    • Two subjects joined by and → plural verb: Ram and Sita are here.
    • Subjects joined by or/nor → verb agrees with nearer subject: Neither he nor they are wrong.
    • Collective nouns → usually singular: The team has won.
    • Indefinite pronouns (everyone, someone, nobody) → singular verb
    Tricky Rules
    • Either/Neither (alone, without or/nor) → singular: Neither is correct.
    • Each/Every/One of → singular: Each student has a book.
    • News, Mathematics, Economics, Physics → singular verb: The news is good.
    • A lot of / Plenty of → depends on noun: A lot of water is. / A lot of students are.
    • The + adjective (The poor, the rich) → plural verb
    • As well as / Along with / Together with → verb agrees with first subject: The manager, along with the staff, is present.

    Subject-Verb Agreement Errors▼
    Wrong ✗Correct ✓Rule
    She don't know.She doesn't know.Singular subject → doesn't
    The team are playing well.The team is playing well.Collective noun → singular
    Everyone have a pen.Everyone has a pen.Everyone → singular
    The news are shocking.The news is shocking.News → always singular
    Neither he nor I is wrong.Neither he nor I am wrong.Verb agrees with "I" (nearer)
    Tense & Verb Form Errors▼
    Wrong ✗Correct ✓Rule
    She is knowing the answer.She knows the answer.Stative verbs don't use continuous
    I have went there.I have gone there.has/have + V3, not V2
    He did not went.He did not go.After did/didn't, use V1
    She use to sing.She used to sing.Past habit → used to
    When he will come, I will leave.When he comes, I will leave.Time clause (when/if) → present tense
    💡
    Stative verbs (no continuous form): know, understand, believe, love, hate, want, need, see, hear, smell, taste, own, have (possession)

    She plays the tennis.She plays tennis.No article before sports/games
    He is an M.A.He is an M.A."M" starts with vowel sound /em/ ✓

    Wrong ✗Correct ✓

    He is married with her.He is married to her.
    I am angry on him.I am angry with him.
    He died from cancer.He died of cancer.

    Grammar Quick Rules — Don't Forget!
    Articles — 3 Golden Rules
    • a = consonant sound (a cat, a university, a European)
    • an = vowel sound (an egg, an hour, an honest man, an MBA)
    • the = specific/unique things (the sun, the Taj Mahal, the first time)
    • No article before: languages, subjects, meals, sports, proper nouns
    Fewer vs Less / Many vs Much
    • Many / Fewer = with countable nouns (many books, fewer students)
    • Much / Less = with uncountable nouns (much water, less time)
    • A number of → plural verb | The number of → singular verb
    • Elder/eldest = only for family members, older/oldest = general
    Common Mistakes to Avoid in Exam
    ❌
    Don't write "Dear Sir/Madam" when the name is given. Use "Dear Mr./Ms. [Surname]" and close with "Yours sincerely" not "Yours faithfully"
    ❌
    Never say "discuss about" — just "discuss". Similarly: "mention about" → "mention", "enter into" → "enter"
    ❌
    Don't use contractions in formal writing — no "can't", "won't", "don't". Write "cannot", "will not", "do not"
    ❌
    Never write "more better" or "most tallest" — double comparatives/superlatives are always wrong
    ❌
    "The news are..." is wrong — news is always singular. Same for physics, mathematics, economics.
    ✅
    In sentence correction MCQs: eliminate wrong options first. Check subject-verb agreement → tense → prepositions → articles in that order.
    ✅
    In spelling MCQs: say the word aloud mentally, look for doubled letters (accommodate, committee, occurred) and -ence vs -ance endings.
    ✅
    For Parts of Speech: test by substituting — if you can replace it with he/she/they, it's a noun. If it modifies a verb, it's an adverb.
    ✅
    Every business letter body needs exactly 3 paragraphs: (1) Why you're writing, (2) Details/explanation, (3) What you expect/closing.
