Linear Motion — Definitions

▼
  • SDistance — total path length (no direction)
  • VDisplacement — shortest path from start to end, has direction
  • SSpeed = Distance / Time
  • VVelocity = Displacement / Time
  • VAcceleration = Change in velocity / Time; can be +ve or −ve
  • SMass — amount of matter, always scalar
  • VForce = mass × acceleration (F = ma)
EQUATIONS OF MOTION (uniform acceleration) v = u + at  |  s = ut + ½at²  |  v² = u² + 2as
Uniform velocity → acceleration = 0. Uniform acceleration → velocity changes linearly.

Newton's Laws of Motion

▼
  • V1st Law (Inertia) — Body remains at rest or in uniform motion unless acted upon by external force. Inertia ∝ mass.
  • V2nd Law — F = ma (or F = dp/dt, rate of change of momentum)
  • V3rd Law — Every action has equal and opposite reaction. Forces act on DIFFERENT bodies.
Inertia is NOT a force — it is a property of matter.

Linear Momentum & Impulse

▼
  • VLinear Momentum (p) = mass × velocity  →  p = mv
  • VImpulse (J) = Force × time = Change in momentum  →  J = FΔt = Δp
  • VLaw of Conservation of Momentum — Total momentum of an isolated system remains constant.
p = mv   |   J = FΔt = mv − mu
Unit of momentum: kg·m/s. Unit of impulse: N·s = kg·m/s.
Impulse = area under Force–Time graph.

Angular Quantities

▼
  • SAngle (θ) — measured in radians; θ = arc length / radius
  • VAngular Velocity (ω) = θ/t = 2πn; direction: right-hand rule
  • VAngular Acceleration (α) = Δω/Δt
  • VLinear velocity v = rω (relation between linear and angular)
  • STime Period (T) = 1/n = 2π/ω
ω = 2πn = 2π/T  |  v = rω  |  ac = v²/r = rω²

In uniform circular motion, speed is constant but velocity changes (direction changes) — so acceleration exists!

Work, Energy, Power — Scalar or Vector?

▼
  • SWork (W) = F·d·cosθ — dot product, always scalar. Unit: Joule (J)
  • SKinetic Energy (KE) = ½mv² — always positive scalar
  • SPotential Energy (PE) = mgh — scalar (can be negative in some contexts)
  • SPower (P) = Work/Time = F·v; Unit: Watt (W)
  • SEnergy — all forms of energy are scalar
W = Fd cosθ  |  KE = ½mv²  |  PE = mgh  |  P = W/t = Fv
Work–Energy Theorem: Net work done = Change in KE → Wnet = ΔKE
Law of Conservation of Energy: Total energy (KE + PE) of an isolated system is constant.

Important Special Cases

▼
  • SIf θ = 0°: W = Fd (maximum work)
  • SIf θ = 90°: W = 0 (force ⊥ displacement — no work done, e.g., circular motion)
  • SIf θ = 180°: W = −Fd (negative work, e.g., friction)
  • S1 HP (Horse Power) = 746 W
  • S1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J

Charge & Coulomb's Law

▼
  • SCharge (Q) — scalar, Unit: Coulomb (C); e = 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ C
  • VCoulomb's Force (F) = k·q₁q₂/r² — vector, acts along line joining charges
  • VElectric Field (E) = F/q = kQ/r² — vector (direction of force on +ve charge)
  • SElectric Potential (V) = W/q = kQ/r — scalar, Unit: Volt
  • SElectric Flux (φ) = E·A·cosθ — scalar, Unit: N·m²/C
  • SElectric Current (I) = Q/t — scalar (conventionally treated scalar in circuits)
F = kq₁q₂/r²  |  k = 9×10⁹ N·m²/C²  |  E = F/q  |  V = kQ/r
Like charges repel, unlike charges attract. Coulomb's law is analogous to Newton's Law of Gravitation.

Ohm's Law & Resistance

▼
  • SResistance (R) = V/I; Unit: Ohm (Ω)
  • SConductance (G) = 1/R; Unit: Siemens (S) or mho
  • SResistivity (ρ) = R·A/L; Unit: Ω·m (property of material)
  • SConductivity (σ) = 1/ρ; Unit: S/m or (Ω·m)⁻¹
R = ρL/A  |  V = IR  |  G = 1/R  |  σ = 1/ρ

Heat Capacity & Thermal Conductivity

▼
  • SHeat Capacity (C) = Q/ΔT; Unit: J/K or J/°C
  • SSpecific Heat Capacity (c) = Q/(mΔT); Unit: J/(kg·K)
  • SSpecific heat of water = 4200 J/(kg·K) — highest of common substances
  • SThermal Conductivity (k) — ability of material to conduct heat; Unit: W/(m·K)
  • SCoefficient of Linear Expansion (α): ΔL = L₀αΔT
Q = mcΔT  |  Q/t = kA(ΔT)/d  |  ΔL = L₀αΔT
Metals have HIGH thermal conductivity. Non-metals (wood, glass) have LOW conductivity — good insulators.

Refraction, Snell's Law & TIR

▼
  • SRefractive Index (n) = c/v = sin i / sin r — scalar, dimensionless
  • SAbsolute RI — measured with respect to vacuum/air
  • SRelative RI — ratio of RI of two media: ₁n₂ = n₂/n₁
  • SCritical Angle (C): sin C = 1/n (light going from denser to rarer)
  • VTIR occurs when: (1) light travels from denser to rarer, (2) angle of incidence > critical angle
Snell's Law: n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂  |  sin C = n₂/n₁ = 1/n (for air)
TIR is the principle behind optical fibres, diamond brilliance, and mirages.

Optical Fibre

▼
  • SConstruction: Core (high RI) + Cladding (low RI) + Protective jacket
  • VPrinciple: Total Internal Reflection — light bounces along fibre without escaping
  • STypes: Single-mode (thin core, less loss) and Multi-mode (thicker core)
Applications
  • SHigh-speed internet & telecommunications (data as light pulses)
  • SMedical endoscopy (viewing inside body)
  • SSensors, military communication, decorative lighting
Optical fibre has zero electromagnetic interference, low signal loss, and very high bandwidth.

LASER

▼
  • SLASER = Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation
  • SProperties: Monochromatic (single wavelength), Coherent (same phase), Directional (parallel beam), High intensity
  • SPrinciple: Population inversion → Stimulated emission → Amplification
Applications
  • SSurgery (laser eye surgery, cutting), Barcode scanning, CD/DVD, Welding
  • SRangefinding, holography, fibre-optic communication
Ordinary light is incoherent & spreads out. LASER is coherent & stays as a narrow beam.

Sound Waves — Basics

▼
  • SAudible range: 20 Hz – 20,000 Hz (human ear)
  • SInfrasonic: below 20 Hz (earthquakes, elephants)
  • SUltrasonic: above 20,000 Hz (bats, dolphins)
  • SSpeed of sound in air ≈ 340 m/s at room temperature
  • SSound travels fastest in solids, slowest in gases

Ultrasonic Waves

▼
  • SProduction: Piezoelectric effect (quartz crystal) or Magnetostriction
  • SProperties: High frequency, high energy, travel in straight lines, reflected well
Applications
  • SMedical: Ultrasonography (SONAR imaging of organs), fetal scanning
  • SIndustry: SONAR (detect submarines, fish, depth), Non-destructive testing (NDT) of metals
  • SCleaning (ultrasonic cleaners), Drilling, Welding of plastics
  • SCommunication under water
Bats use echolocation (ultrasonic) to navigate in the dark.

Reverberation & Sabine's Formula

▼
  • SEcho: Reflected sound heard separately; minimum distance for echo = 17 m (at 340 m/s)
  • SReverberation: Persistence of sound due to multiple reflections after source stops
  • SReverberation Time (T): Time for sound intensity to fall to one-millionth of original value (60 dB drop)
  • SAbsorption Coefficient (a): Ratio of sound absorbed to sound incident; a = 1 for open window (perfect absorber)
SABINE'S FORMULA T = 0.161 × V / (ΣaS)
T = Reverberation time (s), V = Volume of hall (m³), a = absorption coeff, S = surface area (m²)
Ideal reverberation time for speech: ~0.5 s. For music: 1–2 s. Carpets, curtains, audience reduce reverberation.
Open window is the unit of absorption — 1 m² open window = absorption coefficient of 1.
